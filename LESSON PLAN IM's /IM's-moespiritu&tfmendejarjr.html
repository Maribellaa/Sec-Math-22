WHAT IS IN

\(\qquad\) The quadratic formula, \(x=\dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}\), is derived from standard form of quadratic equation, \(ax^2+bx+c=0\) by applying the method of completing the square shown below.

Steps Illustrative
1. Place the constant term on the right side of the equation. All the terms with unknowns are on the left side. \[ax^2+bx+c=0 \to ax^2+bx=-c\]
2. Divide each term of the equation with the numerical coefficient of x^2 if necessary. \[\dfrac{ax^2+bx}{a}=\dfrac{-c}{a} \to x^2+\frac{bx}{a}=\dfrac{-c}{a}\]
3. Get the numerical coefficient of x, divide it by 2 and square it. Add the result to both sides of the equation \[\dfrac{1}{2} \left( \dfrac{b}{2a} \right) = \dfrac{b}{2a} \to \left( \dfrac{b}{2a} \right)^2 = \dfrac{b^2}{4a^2}\]
4. Factor the perfect square trinomial. \[ x^2 + \dfrac{bx}{a} + \dfrac{b^2}{4a^2} = \dfrac{-c}{a} + \dfrac{b^2}{4a^2} \to \left( x + \dfrac{b}{2a} \right)^2 = \dfrac{b^2-4ac}{4a^2}\]
5. Extract the square root from both sides. Two values will be obtained for the right side of the equation. \[ x+\dfrac{b}{2a} = \pm \sqrt {\dfrac{b^2-4ac}{4a^2}} \to x+\dfrac{b}{2a} = \pm \dfrac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \]
6. Solve for x by transposing \(\dfrac{b}{2a}\) on the right of the equation \[ x = \pm \dfrac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} -\dfrac{b}{2a}\]
7. Combine like terms on the right side. \[ x = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \]

Example

Solve for the real roots of each equation using quadratic formula

  1. \(x^2-2x-19=0\)

\(\qquad\) Solution:

\(\qquad\) \(a=1\) \(b=-2\) \(c=-19\)

\[ \begin{aligned} x & = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} && \text{Quadratic Formula} \\ x&= \dfrac{-(-2) \pm \sqrt{(-2)^2-4(1)(-19)}}{2(1)} && \text{Substitute the values of a, b and c} \\ x&= \dfrac{2 \pm \sqrt{4+76}}{2} && \text{Simplify} \\ x&= \dfrac{2 \pm \sqrt{80}}{2} && \text{Evaluate the square root, if possible} \\ x&= \dfrac{2 \pm 4\sqrt{5}}{2} \\ x&= \dfrac{2( 1 \pm 2\sqrt{5})}{2} && \text{Simplify} \\ x&= 1 \pm 2\sqrt{5} \end{aligned} \]

  1. \(2x^2+6x=-3\)

\(\qquad\) Solution:

\(\qquad\) When \(2x^2+6x=-3\) is changed to standard form, we have \(2x^2+6x+3=0\). The value of \(a=2\), \(\qquad\) \(b=6\), and \(c=3\)

\[ \begin{aligned} x & = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} && \text{Quadratic Formula} \\ x&= \dfrac{-6 \pm \sqrt{(6)^2-4(2)(3)}}{2(2)} && \text{Substitute the values of a, b and c} \\ x&= \dfrac{-6 \pm \sqrt{36-24}}{4} && \text{Simplify} \\ x&= \dfrac{-6 \pm \sqrt{12}}{4} && \text{Evaluate the square root, if possible} \\ x&= \dfrac{-6 \pm 4\sqrt{(4)(3)}}{2} \\ x&= \dfrac{-6\pm 2\sqrt{3})}{2} && \text{Simplify} \\ x&= \dfrac{-3 \pm \sqrt{3}}{2} \end{aligned} \]

\(\qquad\) The roots are \(\dfrac{-3 \pm \sqrt{3}}{2}\)

3.\(2x^2+3x-27=0\)

\(\qquad\) Solution:

\(\qquad\) \(a=2\), \(b=3\), and \(c=-27\)

\[ \begin{aligned} x & = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} && \text{Quadratic Formula} \\ x&= \dfrac{-3 \pm \sqrt{(3)^2-4(2)(-27)}}{2(2)} && \text{Substitute the values of a, b and c} \\ x&= \dfrac{-3 \pm \sqrt{9-(-26)}}{4} && \text{Simplify} \\ x&= \dfrac{-3 \pm \sqrt{225}}{4} && \text{Evaluate the square root, if possible} \\ x&= \dfrac{-3 \pm 15}{4} \\ x&= \dfrac{-3 + 15}{4} \qquad x=\dfrac{-3 -15}{4} && \text{Simplify} \\ x&= \dfrac{12}{4} \qquad x=\dfrac{-18}{4} \\ x&= 3 \qquad x=\dfrac{-9}{2} \end{aligned} \]

  1. \(2x^2+9x=-10\)

\(\qquad\) Solution

\(\qquad\) When \(2x^2+9x=-10\) is changed to standard form, we have \(2x^2+9x+10=0\). The value of \(\qquad\) \(a=2\), \(b=9\), and \(c=10\)

\[ \begin{aligned} x& = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} && \text{Quadratic Formula} \\ x&= \dfrac{-9 \pm \sqrt{(9)^2-4(2)(10)}}{2(2)} && \text{Substitute the values of a, b and c} \\ x&= \dfrac{-9 \pm \sqrt{81-80}}{4} && \text{Simplify} \\ x&= \dfrac{-9 \pm \sqrt{1}}{4} && \text{Evaluate the square root, if possible} \\ x&= \dfrac{-9 \pm 1}{4} \\ x&= \dfrac{-9+1}{4}, \qquad x=\dfrac{-9-1}{4} && \text{Simplify} \\ x&= \dfrac{-8}{4}, \qquad x=\dfrac{-10}{4} \\ x&= -2, \qquad x=\dfrac{-5}{2} \end{aligned} \]

  1. \(2x^2-3x+4=0\)

\(\qquad\) Solution:

\(\qquad\) \(a=2\), \(b=-3\), and \(c=4\)

\[ \begin{aligned} x & = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} && \text{Quadratic Formula} \\ x&= \dfrac{-(-3) \pm \sqrt{(-3)^2-4(2)(4)}}{2(2)} && \text{Substitute the values of a, b and c} \\ x&= \dfrac{3 \pm \sqrt{-23}}{4} && \text{Simplify} \end{aligned} \]

\(\qquad\) Notice that the radicand is negative. It indicates that the roots are non-real.

\[ \begin{aligned} x & = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} && \text{Quadratic Formula} \\ x&= \dfrac{-(3.05) \pm \sqrt{(3.05)^2-4(-16)(9.14)}}{2(-16)} && \text{Substitute the values of a, b and c} \\ x&= \dfrac{-3.05 \pm \sqrt{9.3025+584.96}}{2(-16)} && \text{Simplify} \\ x&= \dfrac{-3.05 \pm \sqrt{594.2625}}{-32} && \text{Evaluate the square root, if possible} \\ x&= \dfrac{-3.05 \pm 24.38}{-32} \\ x&= -0.67, \qquad x= 0.86 \end{aligned} \]
